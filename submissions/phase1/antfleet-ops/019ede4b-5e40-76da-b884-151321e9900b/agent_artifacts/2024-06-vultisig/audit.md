# Audit: 2024-06-vultisig

## Theft of Unclaimed Users' Trading Fees and Denial of Service in `claim`
- Location: `src/ILOPool.sol` : `claim`
- Mechanism: The `claim` function operates on a single aggregated Uniswap V3 position held by the `ILOPool` contract for all users. When a user claims, the contract calls `pool.collect()`, which retrieves ALL accumulated trading fees for the entire aggregated position since the last collection. However, the contract only calculates the specific fee share (`fees0`, `fees1`) belonging to the claiming user based on their individual liquidity. It then transfers the user's net payout (`amount0`) to the user, and sends the entire remaining collected balance (`amountCollected0 - amount0`) to the `feeTaker`. Because `amountCollected` includes fees generated by OTHER users' liquidity, the `feeTaker` incorrectly receives fees that belong to users who haven't claimed yet.
- Impact: The `feeTaker` will drain trading fees that rightfully belong to other users. Consequently, the contract's internal token balance will be depleted of those fees. When the remaining users attempt to claim their positions later, the transactions will revert due to insufficient token balance in the contract, permanently locking their liquidity and unclaimed fees.

## Whitelist and Contribution Cap Bypass via Intermediate Contracts
- Location: `hardhat-vultisig/contracts/Whitelist.sol` : `checkWhitelist`
- Mechanism: The whitelist eligibility and per-address ETH contribution cap checks are strictly gated by the condition `from == _pool`. If a user purchases VULT tokens through a Uniswap Router or a custom intermediary contract (where the router/contract is set as the swap recipient), the initial transfer from the pool to the router triggers the check, applying the cap to the router's address. The subsequent transfer from the router to the final user has `from == router`, which fails the `from == _pool` condition and completely bypasses the whitelist and cap logic.
- Impact: Users can entirely bypass the per-address contribution cap and whitelist requirements by routing their swaps through a whitelisted intermediate contract (such as a standard Uniswap Router) or by deploying their own simple wrapper contracts. If a common router is whitelisted, all users share its cap or bypass it entirely; if users deploy custom wrappers, they can isolate the cap per contract rather than per user, rendering the whitelist mechanism ineffective.
